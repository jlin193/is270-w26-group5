Technical Skill Snapshot:

What does it take to start working in DAMs? While we listed some information based on learnings from Yvonne, we also analyzed different job descriptions in the field.

These are real job descriptions posted in 2026, covering a wide range of library types (academic, corporate, museum, etc). Each listing is entry-level, requiring less than 1 year of experience in the field.

  • Instruction and research: Scholarly Communication Librarian @ The University at Albany (Sourced through the Association for Information Science & Technology (ASIS&T))
  • Museum cataloging: Authority Control and Bibliographic Maintenance Librarian @ The Getty (Sourced through the Getty)
  • Library collections: Born Digital Collections Coordinator @ The Library of Virginia (Sourced through Virginia Jobs)
  • Corporate: Digital Asset Specialist @ Best Buy (Sourced through Best Buy)

Skills expected at entry level

Hard skills (technical skills):

  • Experience in / understanding of:
    • File or records management
    • Data entry
    • Metadata standards
    • Art handling, library, and/or museum environments
  • Some familiarity with:
    • Use cases and characteristics of different file formats
    • Digital asset and/or media asset management systems
    • Copyright and intellectual property laws related to digital assets

Soft skills (culture-fit skills):

  • Attention to detail
  • Excellent communication skills, verbal and written
  • Project planning, management, and coordination skills
  • Teamwork, collaboration, and interpersonal skills

What to expect from interviewing

  1. Phone Screen
    2. This step is conducted by a recruiter/HR rep. In this process, you’ll learn more details about the role, and they’ll learn high-level details about you, like your professional/academic background and why you’re interested in the company.
  2. Meet the manager
    3. Next up is a chat with the hiring manager you’ll be directly reporting to. They will be assessing your skills more closely, and also ask other behavioral questions about your approach to work. Sometimes, other members of the team you’d work with may be present during this conversation.
  3. Case study or technical assignment
    4. In many technology-focused and private industry roles, interviews will involve a case study. Some are take-home assignments, and some are done live during an interview with the hiring manager.
  4. Onsite interview
    5. Since the pandemic, many companies conduct their interviews virtually over Zoom. However, academic and museum settings will most likely invite you on-site, where you’ll see the premises and meet other members of the team.

Not all roles will require multi-step interviews—they’re more common in private/corporate settings. At the very least, expect a phone screening and interview with the hiring manager.


How is AI impacting the future of the work?

“Currently, it’s hard to tell. Keeping up with LLMs and their use cases in cultural heritage is helpful, but at this time, The Huntington has not adopted an AI policy as such.”

- Yvonne Lee

When it comes to GenAI use in GLAM institutions, we’re presently in a transitional period. However, there are certain areas in which AI could be (or already is) having an impact:

  • Cataloging and other forms of metadata generation.
  • Enabling search and discovery of collections.
  • Supporting and carrying out research.
  • Public engagement and crowdsourcing.
  • Increasingly used for automated migration and metadata tagging, as datasets only become larger (van Strien et al.).

Complying with legal policies, especially in copyright/licensing and privacy safeguards, are of keen importance to GLAM work. Read more about how GenAI affects The Huntington.
