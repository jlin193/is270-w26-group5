Assignment 5: Guidelines Document for Generative AI Use


Organizational Analysis and Ecological Framing

The Huntington, a research institution located in San Marino, CA, was founded in 1919 as a nonprofit to support creative and inclusive scholarship and community engagement (“About The Huntington”). As one of the largest, most well-known research institutions in the world, it faces some challenges as a uniquely multidisciplinary and multi-faceted organization.

Heather Hart, Vice President for Digital and Technology Solutions, is currently working on writing and implementing guidelines for responsible AI use at The Huntington, and provided valuable insight on the institution’s current practices. She explained that an “ecological perspective” is inherent to work functions across the organization due to its numerous divisions, which include the botanical gardens, a research library, arts galleries, and administrative operations. Designing a specific, yet flexible set of guidelines to navigate new technology is imperative due to the unique impact it will have on each department’s current and potential uses (see Figure 1). The stakeholders in drafting AI policy would include the following (Office of Communications and Marketing):

  • In-house legal counsel
  • President and senior staff
  • Trustees
  • Conversant stakeholders advocating for specific use-cases

Survey of Current and Potential AI-led Transformations

Institutionally supported tools for general use include CoPilot (Microsoft), Google Gemini, ChatGPT, and Claude (Anthropic). Other tools utilized by other departments include Grammarly (writing and editing tool), Canva AI (imagine and video editing tools), Fireflies.ai (notetaking/scribe tool), and Descript (audio and video transcription). Hart describes current AI use at The Huntington as “not anything groundbreaking,” and minimal. See the chart below for a mindful analysis of how AI is changing the ecological landscape.

Challenges

Initially, any AI use at The Huntington had to be documented: tagged on a Digital Asset Management (DAM) system, cited as a source in reports, saved with different “original” vs “AI” versions, and so on. However, this created a massive documentation burden on employees, which contradicts the purpose of AI use in the workplace: efficiency and convenience. Other challenges include using language specific enough to convey accurate-enough information, but flexible enough to provide guidance coverage across all divisions. Given this context, the following list serves as a temporary set of Guidelines for Responsible AI Use at The Huntington, to be revised after one year of implementation.


Solutions

  1. Legal compliance, privacy, and security
    Adhere to federal and state legal requirements for privacy and permissions, as well as other legal policies at The Huntington. AI cannot be used to make hiring decisions or draft legal language. For more guidance and information, read the resources below and/or reach out to The Huntington’s in-house legal counsel.
    2. a. California Code of Regulations: Employment Regulations Regarding Automated-Decision Systems
    b. The Huntington Library Rights and Permissions
    c. Citation Guidelines for Materials Held by the Huntington Library
    d. US Copyright Office - Copyright and Artificial Intelligence
  1. Quality and bias control
    Understand the limitations in the potential for discrimination, bias, and inaccuracy. Keep a “human in the loop” and always review AI-generated or AI-altered content before publishing, sharing, or implementing by applying the CRAAP Test: currency, relevance, authority, accuracy, and purpose (Van Kampen).
  1. Approved tools
    Our Digital and Technology Solutions department provides a range of approved GenAI tools with contractual protections for use in The Huntington work. Please use the listed tools to best comply with security and privacy protection measures.
    2. - Microsoft CoPilot
    - Google Gemini
    - ChatGPT
    - Claude (Anthropic)
  1. Transparency
    When using AI to create something new (e.g. generate an image, write social media captions, structure a workshop/program) for external or internal use, it must be documented, reported, and/or cited appropriately. When using AI to edit or modify a resource, its use does not need to be reported. See more specific guidelines on Retouching and Modification Requirements for Creative Content by Getty Images.
