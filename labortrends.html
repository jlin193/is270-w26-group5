Labor Trends

Based on impressionistic observations of different job listings (and some anecdotal evidence in interviewing for these positions), DAMs work standards are a mixed bag.

Entry-level roles—especially at private companies—are often temporary and/or contract-only, which often means that the jobs come without healthcare coverage and other benefits. In academic and museum institutions, contract work is very common, but there are highly competitive full-time, benefits-eligible positions as well.

While GLAM work is largely female-dominated, institutions are still often white (and male)-led. Disparities become more pronounced in more corporate environments, particularly in technology and finance.

Growing or shrinking?

Expectations for technological literacy for everyone are growing. Current decision-making in DAMs to keep outdated data accessible by any means can lead to “technical debt,” an accumulation of system updates, “long-term consequences of decisions made,” and resource management that could lead to future costs. This proactive theory accounts for the demands for “responsible digital stewardship” in the archiving field and that “all digital projects will accrue debt, and not all debt can be absorbed or “paid off” with future resources” (Joyce, Déirdre, et al.).

The Huntington is actively building more intercommunicative technological infrastructure, reforming the DAMs subfield by meeting regularly with vendors to stay ahead of software updates and patches. Yvonne’s department stays proactive by writing a testing script to flag any anticipated workflow changes and being on call the day of implementation to patch any bugs or glitches.
